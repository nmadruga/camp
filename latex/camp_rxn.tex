A reaction represents a transformation of the model state due to a physical or chemical process that occurs within a phase (gas or \mbox{\hyperlink{camp_aero_phase}{aerosol}}) or across the interface between two phases. In the \mbox{\hyperlink{index}{camp-\/chem}} model, reactions are grouped into \mbox{\hyperlink{camp_mechanism}{mechanisms}}, which are solved over time-\/steps specified by the host model.

The primary function of a reaction in the \mbox{\hyperlink{index}{camp-\/chem}} model is to provide the solver with contributions to the time derivative and \mbox{\hyperlink{struct_jacobian}{Jacobian}} matrix for \mbox{\hyperlink{camp_species}{chemical species}} concentrations based on the current model state described in a {\ttfamily \mbox{\hyperlink{structcamp__camp__state_1_1camp__state__t}{camp\+\_\+camp\+\_\+state\+::camp\+\_\+state\+\_\+t}}} object.

Specific reaction types extend the abstract {\ttfamily \mbox{\hyperlink{structcamp__rxn__data_1_1rxn__data__t}{camp\+\_\+rxn\+\_\+data\+::rxn\+\_\+data\+\_\+t}}} type and generally accept a set of reactants and products whose names correspond to \mbox{\hyperlink{camp_species}{chemical species}} names, as well as a set of reaction parameters needed to describe a particular reaction. During initialization, a reaction will have access to its set of parameters as well as the parameters of any \mbox{\hyperlink{camp_species}{species}} and \mbox{\hyperlink{camp_aero_rep}{aerosol phase}} in the \mbox{\hyperlink{index}{camp-\/chem}} model, however this information will not be available during a model run. The information required by the reaction instance to calculate its contribution to the time derivatve and \mbox{\hyperlink{struct_jacobian}{Jacobian}} matrix must therefore be packed into the condensed data arrays of the {\ttfamily \mbox{\hyperlink{structcamp__rxn__data_1_1rxn__data__t}{camp\+\_\+rxn\+\_\+data\+::rxn\+\_\+data\+\_\+t}}} object during intialization.

Henry\textquotesingle{}s Law phase-\/trasfer reactions use equilibrium rate constants that are calculated as\+:

\[ HLC(298K) * e^{C({1/T-1/298})} \]

where $HLC(298K)$ is the Henry\textquotesingle{}s Law constant at 298 K \mbox{[}M $\mbox{Pa}^{-1}$\mbox{]}, $C$ is a constant \mbox{[}K\mbox{]} and $T$ is the temperature \mbox{[}K\mbox{]}. Uptake kinetics are based on the particle effective radius, $r_{eff}$ \mbox{[} $\mbox{m}$\mbox{]}, the condensing species gas-\/phase diffusion coefficient, $D_g$ \mbox{[} $\mbox{m}^2\,\mbox{s}^{-1}$\mbox{]}, its molecular weight $MW$ \mbox{[}kg $\mbox{mol}^{-3}$\mbox{]}, and $N^{*}$, which is used to calculate the mass accomodation coefficient.

Mass accomodation coefficients and condensation rate constants are calculated using the method of Ervans et al. (2003) {\bfseries [Ervens2003]} and references therein. Mass accomodation coefficients ( $\alpha$) are calculated as\+:

\[ \Delta H_{obs} = -10 \times (N^*-1) + 7.53 \times (N^{*2/3}-1) - 0.1 \times 10 \quad (\mbox{kcal}\,\mbox{M}^{-1}) \] \[ \Delta S_{obs} = -13 \times (N^*-1) - 19 \times (N^*-1) + 9.21 \times (N^{*2/3}-1) - 0.1 \times 13 \quad (\mbox{cal}\,\mbox{M}^{-1}\,\mbox{K}^{-1}) \] \[ \frac{\alpha}{1-\alpha} = e^{\frac{-\Delta G^{*}}{RT}} \] If $\Delta H$ and $\Delta S$ are not provided, $\alpha$ is set to 0.\+1 {\bfseries [Zaveri2008]}.

Condensation rate constants are calculated as\+: \[ k_{f} = (\frac{r^2}{3D_g} + \frac{4r}{3 \langle c \rangle \alpha})^{-1} \] where $r$ is the particle radius ( $\mbox{m}$) and $\langle c \rangle $ is the mean speed of the gas-\/phase molecules\+: \[ \langle c \rangle = \sqrt{\frac{8RT}{\pi MW}} \] where $R$ is the ideal gas constant \mbox{[} $\mbox{J}\,\mbox{K}^{-1}\,\mbox{mol}^{-1}$\mbox{]}. The particle radius used to calculate $k_{f}$ is the effective radius \mbox{[} $r_{eff}$\mbox{]}, which is taken as the \char`\"{}least-\/wrong\char`\"{} choice for condensation rates, as it is weighted to surface area {\bfseries [Zender2002]} .
